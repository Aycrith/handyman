Reference Digest to append beneath the prompt

Mantis
Mantis presents itself like a sensory, design-led studio world rather than a conventional agency brochure: the live page exposes weather, time, coordinates, a reel CTA, and emotionally charged lines like “we create feelings,” “activate nerves,” “stimulate memory,” and “excite neurons.” Public design profiles classify it as a minimal 3D studio site with experimental, typography-led, WebGL/Cinema 4D energy and big background imagery. The transferable cue is a branded editorial atmosphere where utility details, oversized type, and immersive art direction feel like one authored system.

SR Seventy
Public design-gallery metadata describes SR Seventy as a dark-mode, 3D, hardware-oriented site about the SR-71, built in the GSAP/WebGL/Three.js ecosystem. Publicly accessible text is thinner here than for the other references, so the safest benchmark to extract is disciplined technical storytelling: one iconic subject, dark aerospace framing, premium motion, and a clean, high-spec presentation instead of clutter or overstimulation.

Henry Heffernan
Henry Heffernan’s portfolio announces itself as an experience (“best viewed on a desktop or laptop computer,” “click start to begin”), and design-gallery profiles classify it as 3D, minimal, retro, and Three.js-based, often in a black-and-white palette. Public reactions also highlight playful, game-like surprise elements, including a DOOM easter egg. The useful benchmark here is nostalgic interface world-building: a site that feels like entering a system, not just scrolling a page.

Dragonfly
Dragonfly is one of the clearest examples of a tightly authored visual system: the official site is terse and controlled, while Awwwards and the Studio Freight case study describe a dark dramatic palette, black backgrounds, a vivid orange accent, experimental scrolling/3D/microinteractions, ASCII-based graphics, custom 16-bit portraits, and a typography pairing of Non Natural Grotesk with Mondwest. Artistically, it blends research-heavy seriousness with irreverence and computational texture. The main lesson is how to make a site feel severe, intelligent, and culturally specific without becoming sterile.

LHBZR
LHBZR’s older work is documented as a unique minimal one-pager with a background visualizer synced to a pumping soundtrack, and related portfolio/case-study pages tag the visual language as experimental, interactive, WebGL-driven, black-and-green, and Swiss-influenced. This makes it a strong benchmark for restraint plus reactivity: tight typography, dark negative space, and a reactive audiovisual center of gravity instead of constant full-screen chaos.

TPLH / Yoichi Kobayashi
TPLH is heavily JS-driven, but external writeups are consistent: the portfolio uses live-rendered generative art as the core of the experience, and the visuals evolve from visit to visit rather than acting as static decoration. Awwwards’ case-study index frames it as an experiment to use all of the creator’s current skills, and trend articles specifically call out the entertaining loading animation. This is a benchmark for code-as-art direction: generative behavior is the identity.

Active Theory
Across multiple versions, Active Theory is consistently described through the same vocabulary: dark/fullscreen presentation, WebGL, 3D, real-time environments, filters/effects, cinematic transitions, interactive intro moments, particle effects, typographic navigation, and, in newer versions, even AI chat and multiplayer notes. This is less a single site than a long-running study in immersive studio identity. The transferable lesson is stagecraft: camera, particles, navigation, and scene transitions all work together as a choreographed performance.

No Hero Studio
No Hero’s official page is unusually explicit about its spatial and sonic intent: “best experienced with sound,” “click & drag,” “look around,” coordinates, audio toggles, and repeated “currently generating” language all push the site toward a navigable control-room fiction. Design-gallery and trend references reinforce that with tags like 3D, gaming, illustration, dark mode, and observations about pixel fonts, glitchy typography, and sci-fi interfaces. The artistic takeaway is a gamified cyber-brutalist aesthetic that feels system-native rather than polished in a generic SaaS way.

Dogstudio
Dogstudio leads with aggressive typographic confidence (“We Make Good Shit”) and frames its practice at the intersection of art, design, and technology. The live site and case-study descriptions emphasize immersive, emotional, culturally oriented experiences rather than purely utilitarian web work. The useful benchmark is authorship: bold verbal/visual attitude, strong editorial presentation of case studies, and motion that feels emotionally intentional rather than merely technically advanced.

Montfort
Montfort is a corporate/commodity brief elevated into a cinematic interactive site. The live page uses “Swipe down / Scroll down to discover” framing, Awwwards records a very restrained blue-and-off-white palette with 3D/gestures/UI-design tags, and public writeups emphasize smooth cinematic transitions, elegant details, distinct visual worlds by section, WebGL, and playful microinteractions. The main benchmark here is how to make a sober business subject feel premium and immersive through pacing, restraint, and sectional world-building.

Adaline
Adaline combines product clarity with a calm environmental metaphor. The official site pairs dense product content with scenic footer assets like hills and dock reflections, while Awwwards describes the concept as “a landscape for thought” composed for clarity and calm. A design roundup goes further and explicitly describes rolling green hills, trees, a misty lake, and a bench behind the interface. The lesson is that a technical SaaS product can still feel spatial, poetic, and serene if the metaphor is consistent and restrained.
